Gent^n

The dry rot in Ships, having lately become a
great national loss, besides an incalculable
disappointment, it therefore becomes an object of more
than common importance, to hit upon some mode of
preventing the same - Imagination sometimes leads us
to attempt the performance of impossibilities, and an
attempt to eradicate, or prevent this evil, may be
considered futile or purely imaginable, having hitherto
baffled the most ingenious, yet it may not be
impossible, and I trust my present ideas, will not
appear to be the result of illguided imagination, and
I feel assured that any attempt, likely to attain so
desirable a purpose, will not be discarded by you. -
The dry rot in Ships, is universally admitted to be
caused by an Insect; if so, the remedy will not oppose
such difficulty; but on the contrary, should it be a
natural defect of the wood, or occasioned by the
partial exclusion of atmospheric air, or some other
cause we cannot immediately account for, still, it
does not argue the same cannot be prevented. - I
never have seen a Ship in this situation, but I
have repeatedly seen Oak Timber, in various
situations

The Hon^ble Commissioners
of His Majesty's Navy

Navy Office 17. July 1812.

Sir,

We desire you will proceed with Mr. Lionel
Lukin to Plymouth as early as possible, and
inspect the state of His Majesty's Ship Queen
Charlotte, which has received very considerable
injury by the dry rot, with a view to ascertain
what measures it may be advisable to adopt to
stop its further progress if possible, by preventing
the vegetation and spreading of the Fungi.

We further desire that your Report on this
subject may be extended to the measures
necessary to prevent this evil in any of His
Majesty's Ships: and that you will make it
either in conjunction with Mr Lukin or
separately as you may find most convenient.

We are, Sir,
Your humble Servants,
