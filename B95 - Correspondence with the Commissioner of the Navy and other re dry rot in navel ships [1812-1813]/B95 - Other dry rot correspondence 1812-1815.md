Dock Yard, Deptford.

Sir

May I request you to order some Trenails to be
sent to me, as follows, viz. one to 6 in each state or time
of preservation to the most perfectly seasoned & fit for use.
At the same time I should like to have a piece of Oak
three or four feet long & four inches thick, in the most
perfectly seasoned state and if the time of gathering
or commencement of [inserted above: its] seasoning can be specified, as
well as in what size it was seasoned, it will be most
convenient. I am not aware whether I am at all
authorized to send thus; but you will, Sir, I hope
excuse me if I am wrong & favour me with the infor-
-mation. I am, Sir, Your very humble Servant

27^th Jan^y 1814

Deptford Dock Yard,

Sir
I duly received your note of the 4^th Jan^y observing that no
Timber has been stacked on the Platform, pedestals &c on
account of the brickwork not being sufficiently firm & I
understood from you in conversation that it was to be stacked
in March. I never thought the brickwork would answer the
purpose & Mr Stone had better perhaps not at all depend
upon it but look to some other support.

8^th March 1815.
I am, Sir, your obed^t humble servant
Ja^s Sowerby

## Page 10

Dear Sir

Knowing that any information on the
subject now under your consideration will be acceptable, I
beg to mention, that some of the Timber used in building Ships
of War I believe some part of the planks, are boiled in a
Temperature above 212 of Fah^t. for the purpose of softening
sufficiently to be bent, it may be worth while enquiring if this
may not occasion a partial decomposition of the Fibre of the Wood
or perhaps after being thus softened the Wood may not be
sufficiently dried before being used, you will likely have an
oportunity of observing those planks which have undergone this
operation of boiling, & how far they have suffered in comparison
with others. I am Dear Sir
very truly Yours
Tho^s Edwards

Bury Court
19^th July 1812

Dear Sir

Presuming on that friendship which your late
noble Brother was inclined to show me and the kind manner of
your expressing yourself when recollecting me so many years
after I had been a visitor to him at Hayes I take the liberty
of asking your advice as to my concerns with your Honourable
Board, (with which no doubt you are well aquainted) on ac=
=count of the prevalence of the mischief caused by the Dryrot.
As I have now rendered real service in saving His Majesties
Ship the Queen Charlotte from condemnation (which would,
in the commonest case be a sufficient reason for demanding
salvage) and in assisting in various communications on the
subject for the greater part of two years without any fee, reward
or even promise to encourage me; may I request your kind advice
as to my persevering (however unfortunately the dreadful state
of these things require it on the part of the Nation; & however
clearly I may see the means of removing the evil) to the hazard
