Private				Navy Office 18^th July 1812

Dear Sir,

I need not repeat to you how much alive
I am to the object of your visit to Plymouth; and I
trust that you will consider the hints that I am
about to give you, as proceeding from an ardent wish
that you may have all possible information on the
subject, and that no circumstance, however minute,
may escape your observation, while you are on the spot.
The fact that you mentioned to Sir William Rule,
of timber that has been heated being more subject
to dry rot than other wood, made me enquire into
the case; and I find that it is proved by practice.
The King's Yacht has a galley between decks, and
the parts where it stands were subject to a great
degree of heat, while attending His Majesty at
Weymouth: Upon the planks being removed, some
time after her return, they, as well as the frame which
was exposed to this heat, were found in a complete

state

---
## Page 5

state of decay from the growth of Fungi; while the other
parts of the Vessel were free from vegetation ..
It has been the object of Mr. Lukin to
destroy the Boletus by drying up the moisture by
intense heat: for this purpose, stoves were placed
in the Hold and upon the several decks. - The heat
in the Hold must have been very great, as immersion
would prevent the escape of the air; while that on the
decks, I imagine, was not at a very high degree of
temperature. - Is it to the great heat under water
that you attribute the absence of the plant, or to the
action of salt water? It may be right to mention
that the middle and upper decks were particularly
shrunk by heat and of course admitted Atmospheric
air - Can the state of the Thermometer on the several
decks be ascertained? -
It is not only in this ship that Pitch-pine has
proved more subject to dry rot than other wood, but
also in the Bellepoule Frigate .. I saw some specimens
taken from her, which were in the same state as that
which you saw at this Office .. In fact, from all the
observations and enquiries that I have been enabled

to make

to make, timber grown in America is very subject
to decomposition from that cause ..
I should be sorry to misrepresent any circumstance
or take up only one side of the question - Many ships that
have, to the best of my knowledge, been constructed of
English Oak only, have also been destroyed by Boletus
lachrymans. - On this subject you can gain some
local information: the Foudroyant of 80 Guns built
at Plymouth in 1798 was nearly destroyed by it after
three Years' service - the Ajax, 74, built by contract
in 1798, was also found in the same state after the
like service - and many other instances too numerous
to detail here ..
I beg to direct your attention to the small
chocks through which bolts pass to connect the timbers ..
they strike me not only to be injurious to the circulation
of air between the frames, but also, by confining the
carbonic acid gas which issues from the timber (more
particularly where sap is left) to promote the growth
of vegetation .. These chocks are to my mind, not
only useless in themselves, but injurious ..
It will be well to consider whether the ship

is

---
## Page 6

is sufficiently stripped to ensure her being free from
the Fungi; for a recurrence of the evil, after her
repair, would be extremely unfortunate ..
Some of the Materials (Planks) that have
been taken off are intended to be replaced: it will
be right to ascertain whether they are not so far
infected as to endanger her future safety ..
With every good wish for your pleasant
journey, I remain,

Dear Sir,

Yours very truly
John Knowles

My Dear Sir I recieved yours late last night. I would by no means
venture to fumigate the Vigo at all, as I concieve it will make
the case still more desperate. I venture thus much to M^r Seppings
and you knowing your whole desire is to do good. I shall
remember both his and your instructions and am, with kind
respects to M^r S. yours very faithfully
To J. Knowles Esq^r. Jas Sowerby

## Page 33

Dear Sir
I rec^d your note of the 5^th July. I feel much for
you on account of your loss - it is a sort of trial we must
all undergo, and I hope you will bear it with the resignation due
to the [unclear: decrees?] of [unclear: Fathers?]. I presume I need not trouble Mr
Jones, or his old cloaths man about the Hygrometer, especially
as it is so long since. I sent a bill for colouring
the Hygrometers &c on the 23^d Jan^y and not having seen nor
heard since I supposed all was forgotten; perhaps
you will find it; if not I can send you a copy if
you please. I am much engaged in my profession, so
that I am always at home, where I shall be happy
to see you, or your friends at any time.
I am Dear Sir
Yours most truly
Ja^s Sowerby

1815 July 6.
2 Mead Place
Lambeth

## Page 3

Private

7
15 July 1812

Dear Sir

Since I did myself the pleasure
of calling upon you with M^r Rackett
I have frequently stated both at the
Admiralty and this Board the necessity
of taking your opinion on the subject
of the dry-rot in the Queen Charlotte.
I find at length, that they have
[inserted above: you to attend]
summoned them tomorrow at 3 o'Clock
If you could call upon me before
that hour I shall be glad to have
some conversation with you. Any
one will shew you my room in
this office. M^r Rackett has left
London. I am with respect

Navy Office
Wednesday

Y^r obedi^t Serv^t
John Knowles

Surveyors of [unclear: Lukin?]
[unclear: Woolwich?]
