# Dry Rot and Timber Decay in British Naval and Scientific Records (1665-1869)

## Research Question

The 1823 Encyclopedia Britannica article on "Dry Rot" claims the disease was known "at some period... of Sir John Pringle's presidency of the Royal Society of London" (1772-1778), but "perhaps was not known, certainly not by its present name, beyond the middle of the last century." We searched the complete OCR archive of the Philosophical Transactions (8,128 articles, 1665-1869) to trace the emergence of dry rot awareness and terminology.

## Key Findings

1. **The term "dry rot" for cordage** dates to at least **1725** (Gray, naval pamphlet) — decades before its application to timber.

2. **The term "dry rot" applied to timber** first appears in **1774** (House of Commons Journals — a church destroyed), with Hartley confirming in 1776 that it was "commonly called by the term Dry-Rot" and that "we do not know the real cause of it."

3. **In the Philosophical Transactions**, the term first appears in **1778** (Mahon, fireproofing paper), used without definition. No Phil Trans article on dry rot appeared during Banks's 42-year presidency (1778-1820), despite Banks testifying as an expert on dry rot in court (1785).

4. **The organism *Boletus lachrymans*** is absent from Withering's comprehensive 1776 British flora (~80 fungi species) but present in his 1792 2nd edition. By 1797 Sowerby describes it as "much too common in England."

5. **The Society of Arts** offered no dry rot prize from 1759 to 1780, but established a gold medal for it by 1783 — never awarded through at least 1789.

6. **Pepys's 1684 toadstools** represent native wet/brown rot from neglect, not *Serpula lacrymans*. The "perished to powder" symptom is shared by multiple native European brown rot fungi.

7. **The Hartley passage (1776)** is the most analytically important source: he observes the wainscot puzzle (dry rot in open rooms contradicting ventilation theory), admits ignorance of the cause, and proposes renaming it "Wet-Rot."

8. **Sowerby's 1812 Navy Board archive** (NHM B95-B98, fully analyzed April 2026) reveals that the naval dry rot crisis involved **different fungi** from the building dry rot. His full dockyard species list (~25 species) shows *B. lachrymans* was "seldom" found; the primary ship attackers were *Boletus hybridus* (= modern *Fibroporia vaillantii*, a conifer-specialist brown rot polypore) and *Xylostroma giganteum* (oak-specific). Sowerby explicitly distinguished ship fungi from building dry rot in his unpublished drafts: *B. lachrymans* was "found more in damp building on shore."

9. **The B. hybridus taxonomy is resolved.** *Boletus hybridus* Sowerby (EF Tab. 289) = *Fibroporia vaillantii* (DC.) Parmasto 1968, confirmed by all major nomenclatural databases. This is a brown-rot polypore in order Polyporales — taxonomically remote from *Serpula lacrymans* (order Boletales). The previous suggestion of *Donkioporia expansa* was incorrect.

## Evidence Timeline

### Phase 0: Pepys and the Toadstool Evidence (1679-1690)

The earliest detailed British account of fungal growth on naval timber comes from Samuel Pepys's *Memoires Relating to the State of the Royal Navy of England* (printed 1690), covering the decade ending December 1688.

**Context**: Pepys left his post as Secretary of the Admiralty in April 1679, leaving the Navy in excellent condition (83 ships, 18,323 men). When he returned in May 1684, the Navy had deteriorated catastrophically under a Commission: only 24 ships at sea, stores worth only £5,000, and repairs estimated at £120,000. The 30 new ships built by Parliament were in "ill plight."

**The Toadstool Passage (p.87)**:
> "Their *Holds* not clean'd nor air'd, but (for want of *Gratings* and opening their *Hatches* and *Scuttles*) suffer'd to heat and moulder, till I have with my own Hands gather'd **Toad-stools** growing in the most considerable of them, **as big as my Fists**. Some not once *heel'd* or *breem'd* since their building, but expos'd in hot weather to the *Sun*, broiling in their *Buttocks* and elsewhere, for want of cooling with Water"

**Timber Reduced to Powder (p.18)**:
> "their Neighbouring *Timbers* in many places **perish'd to powder**, to the rendring them unable with safety to admit of being *breem'd*, for fear of taking *Fire*; and their whole *sides* more disguis'd by *Shot-boards* nail'd, and *Plaisters* of *Canvas* pitch'd thereon (for hiding their Defects, and keeping them above Water) than has been usually seen upon the coming in of a *Fleet* after a *Battle*"

**The Commissioners' Report to the King (p.93)**:
> "how deeply the Ships were **infected** with that evil, by which they were (even in their Thickest stuff) become **rotten and reduc'd to powder**. It seems a matter of too great presumption (without **wholly stripping them**) to undertake against any possible remains or returns of the **said evil**."

**The Official Diagnosis — Neglect, Not Disease (p.86)**:
> "Nought remain'd whereon the same could with any appearance of *Consequence* be charg'd, save the plain *Omission* of the necessary and ordinary *Cautions* us'd for the preserving of *New-built Ships*."

Pepys also investigated and rejected three popular explanations for the decay (pp.60-65):
1. **Hastiness of building** — refuted: the quickest-built ship took 9 months on the stocks; 23 of the 30 took 2-5 years
2. **Greenness of the timber** — refuted: old Navy ships built with timber standing, cut, and converted in 6 months had lasted far longer
3. **East-Country (Baltic) timber** — refuted: only 500 of 35,000 loads were foreign; ships built entirely with English timber fared no better; a formal conference of "all the Eminent Master Builders in the River of Thames" (April 17, 1686) unanimously endorsed foreign plank

**Analysis for the Serpula Timeline**:

The Pepys evidence is critical for establishing what timber decay looked like in the 1680s Royal Navy:

1. **The toadstools are wet rot, not dry rot.** The conditions Pepys describes — sealed, unventilated, hot, damp holds that were never cleaned or aired — are textbook for wet rot fungi (*Donkioporia expansa*, *Fibroporia vaillantii*, or similar). Large fruiting bodies ("as big as my Fists") growing in ship holds suggest organisms that thrive in sustained high moisture.

2. **The language of infection without a named disease.** The Commissioners use "infected with that evil," "remains or returns of the said evil" — they understood the decay could spread or recur, but had no name for it. The terminology is entirely generic: "Decays," "this Calamity," "that evil."

3. **"Reduc'd to powder" — a symptom of brown rot fungi generally, not exclusively *Serpula lacrymans*.** Both Pepys (p.18) and the Commissioners (p.93) describe timber crumbling to powder. While this is the hallmark of brown cubical rot, multiple **native European fungi** produce identical symptoms, including *Coniophora puteana* (cellar fungus — extremely common in damp buildings), *Laetiporus sulphureus* (chicken of the woods — attacks oak heartwood), *Fibroporia vaillantii* (mine fungus), and *Fomitopsis pinicola* (red-belted conk). All digest cellulose while leaving lignin intact, causing wood to shrink, crack into cubical blocks, and crumble to powder when dry. In the saturated conditions of Pepys's neglected ship holds, native brown rot fungi fully explain the "perished to powder" observation without requiring *Serpula lacrymans* to be present. The fire hazard Pepys notes (timber too dry to safely breem) is consistent with damp-rotted wood that subsequently dried out, not necessarily with active dry rot.

4. **The cause is attributed entirely to neglect.** No biological agent is suspected. Ships were not graved, not heeled, not breemed, not ventilated. The "Cautions" are all about maintenance practice, not about a mysterious new disease.

5. **No awareness of a novel organism or novel behaviour.** Pepys, a Fellow of the Royal Society and President (1684-1686), shows no surprise at the *type* of decay, only at its *extent*. He treats it as the predictable consequence of neglect, not as something new or unprecedented. Crucially, all the decay he describes occurs in **damp, enclosed conditions** — exactly where native brown rot fungi thrive. He never describes the distinctive *Serpula* behaviour: decay spreading to dry timber, across masonry, or through well-maintained buildings. The 1680s naval timber crisis was caused by familiar organisms in neglected ships, not by an invasive species attacking in new ways.

6. **The timber supply question.** Pepys's detailed investigation of English vs. Baltic timber (pp.60-79) and the formal conference of Master Builders establishes that by the 1680s, England was already dependent on foreign plank (100 loads of foreign for every 20 of English). This shift in timber sourcing — from English-grown oak to Baltic imports — may be relevant to the later arrival of *Serpula lacrymans*, which could have travelled with imported timber.

### Phase 0b: The Term "Dry-Rot" in Naval Cordage (1725)

**1725 — Galfridus Gray, *The Royal Navy suitably manned***

> "Also a Method to preserve the Rigging of the Royal Navy, the **Cordage from Dry-Rot** and the **Sails from Mildew**; the **Two great Enemies** in length of Time &c. And Timber may be so Managed as to endure as long again."

This is the **earliest known use of the term "Dry-Rot"** found in this research — but it is applied to **CORDAGE (rope), not timber**. Timber is mentioned separately and dry rot is not applied to it.

Significance:
1. The **term existed decades before its application to timber**. "Dry-Rot" originally described rope/fibre deterioration — becoming brittle and powdery while appearing sound. A real phenomenon, but nothing to do with *Serpula lacrymans*.
2. The term later **migrated from cordage to timber**, probably when a similar decay pattern (brittle, powdery, apparently dry) appeared in wood. This migration likely coincided with the spread of *Serpula lacrymans*.
3. "Dry-Rot" and "Mildew" were already **paired as named, recognised conditions** of naval stores by 1725.
4. The 1823 Britannica claim that the disease "was not known... by its present name, beyond the middle of the last century" **probably refers to the timber application**, not the pre-existing term for cordage.

Source: Gray, Galfridus. *The Royal Navy suitably manned* (S.n., 1725). ECCO, Gale doc CW0109367588.

### Phase 0c: Building Manuals Before Dry Rot (1708-1726)

**1726 — Richard Neve, *The City and Country Purchaser, and Builder's Dictionary*, 2nd edition**

An alphabetical dictionary of building terms — **no entry for "dry rot."** The only rot reference is the same Alder passage found in Scamozzi/Leyburn 1708: "where it is sometimes wet, sometimes dry, it rots immediately." Published the same year Gray (1725) used "Dry-Rot" for naval cordage — the building trade had no equivalent concept for timber.

Source: Neve, Richard. *The City and Country Purchaser* (London: D. Browne, 1726). Making of the Modern World, Gale doc U0100616968.

**1708 — Scamozzi/Brown/Leyburn, *The Mirror of Architecture*, 5th edition**

A widely-used building manual, the only mention of rot in the entire text:

> "Timber ought not (especially Oak) to be wrought very wet, nor too dry; for too wet makes it more apt to rot, and too dry, more hard to work."

This is the sum total of what British builders needed to know about timber decay in 1708. No "dry rot." No fungi. No paradoxical decay of dry timber. No warnings about enclosed spaces, plaster moisture, or infectious spreading. Just the ancient, simple relationship: wet timber rots.

Compare to Mahon 1778 (70 years later): builders must keep materials "most perfectly dry before the boards are laid, **for fear of the dry-rot**." A completely new hazard had entered British building experience.

Source: Scamozzi, V., J. Brown, W. Leyburn. *The Mirror of Architecture*, 5th ed. (London: John Sprint, 1708), p.115. ECCO, Gale doc CW0106461605.

### Phase 1: Ship Ventilation and Putrid Air (1742-1755)

The earliest Phil Trans discussions of timber decay in ships frame it entirely as an atmospheric/chemical problem.

**1742 — Richard Mead, "An Account of Mr. Sutton's Invention" (jstor-104139)**
- Sutton's invention for "Changing the Air in the Hold, and other close Parts of a Ship"
- Framework: confined air loses its "Elasticity or Springiness," becomes "poisonous and deadly"
- No mention of timber decay; focus is entirely on crew health

**1742 — William Watson, "Some Observations upon Mr. Sutton's Invention" (jstor-104147)**
- Extends Mead's account with observations on putrid air
- Links "Bulge-water" putrefaction to both crew illness and ship damage
- Notes "the Putrefaction is prevented" by air's cooling of blood — same vocabulary applied to organic decay generally
- Key passage: air "robbed of this valuable Property... must produce the most putrid, if not pestilential" effects

**1755 — Stephen Hales, "An Account of the Great Benefit of Ventilators" (jstor-104948)**
- **The critical passage**: "the preserving much longer from decay the timbers of ships laid up in ordinary in harbour. For, as we are assured by daily experience, that **the decay is wholly owing to damp, close, confined, putrid, corroding air**; so the only remedy for this evil is the frequently changing the air among the timbers, by plentiful ventilations"
- First explicit recognition in Phil Trans that ship timber decay is a serious, ongoing problem
- Still entirely miasmatic framework — ventilation is the cure
- References Captain Thomson of the Success Frigate

### Phase 2: Pringle's Antiseptic Framework (1749-1776)

John Pringle's research on putrefaction provided the dominant theoretical framework for understanding decay of all organic matter.

**1749 — John Pringle, "Further Experiments on Substances Resisting Putrefaction" (jstor-104694)**
- Tests various "antiseptics" (salt of hartshorn, nitre, chamomile) against putrefaction of organic matter
- Concludes "whatever is preservative of Flesh will be generally antiseptic"
- Though focused on animal matter, provided the scientific vocabulary applied to timber

**1749 — John Pringle, "A Continuation of the Experiments on Substances Resisting Putrefaction" (jstor-104691)**
- Extended antiseptic experiments
- Pringle becomes THE authority on putrefaction — explains why the 1823 Britannica associates awareness of dry rot with his presidency

**1774 — Joseph Priestley to Pringle, "On the noxious Quality of the Effluvia of putrid Marshes" (jstor-105987)**
- Directly addressed to Pringle, referencing his "excellent treatise on the diseases of the army"
- Demonstrates Pringle's centrality to the putrefaction discourse
- Tests putrid air on living organisms

**1776 — James Cook to Pringle, "The Method taken for preserving the Health of the Crew" (jstor-106286)**
- Cook's methods for the Resolution: drying, airing, smoking ships with gunpowder and vinegar
- Addresses Pringle as PRS — places Cook's practical experience within Pringle's antiseptic framework
- Sugar as "antiscorbutic," ventilation, keeping "the ship clean and dry between decks"
- No awareness of biological agents — prevention is environmental control

### Phase 2b: Hales Connects Ships and Buildings (1758)

**1758 — Stephen Hales, *A Treatise on Ventilators* (expanded from the 1755 Phil Trans paper)**

> "Where the Earth under the Ground-floors of Houses is damp, and close confined, it not only rots in a few Years the **Joice and Boards of the Floors**, but also, by its **ascending putrid Vapour**, the Very **Wainscot** of those Rooms, **as it does the upper Works of Ships**: Whereas, when the Earth under Ground-floors is **dry, they do not rot**, notwithstanding the Air is close confined under those Floors"

This is the **first explicit connection of ship timber rot to building timber rot**. Hales sees the same phenomenon in both environments — rot ascending from damp conditions through structural timber. Key points:

1. **Rot spreading upward through buildings** — from damp earth, through joists, to wainscoting. This ascending, spreading behaviour is consistent with *Serpula lacrymans* but also with native wet rot in sustained damp conditions.
2. **Ships and buildings linked** — "as it does the upper Works of Ships." The bridge between the naval problem (Hales 1755) and the building problem (Mahon 1778) is here in 1758.
3. **Control observation** — dry ground = no rot, even in confined air. The rot requires moisture to initiate.
4. **Still no name, still no fungi** — just "rots" and "putrid Vapour." Twenty years before "dry-rot" enters the vocabulary.
5. **Ventilation as remedy** — Hales's entire framework: change the air, remove the putrid vapour, stop the rot.

Source: Hales, Stephen. *A Treatise on Ventilators* (London: R. Manby, 1758). Making of the Modern World, Gale doc U0101083778.

### Phase 3: The Term Applied to Timber (1774-1778)

**1774 — Journals of the House of Commons (Anno 14 Georgii III Regis)**

The earliest confirmed use of "dry rot" applied to **timber** found in this research:

> "That the **Walls have imbibed the Damp**, by which **a dry Rot has destroyed the Floors and Pews**, and likewise the **Timbers which support the Building**, by which the Whole is become dangerous; and it is absolutely necessary the same should be **re-built**."

A petition or report to Parliament about a church requiring demolition due to dry rot. The mechanism — damp walls causing dry rot in floors, pews, and structural timbers — is textbook *Serpula lacrymans*: moisture in masonry enabling fungal attack throughout a building.

Significance:
1. **1774 — during Pringle's presidency** (1772-1778). Confirms the 1823 Britannica claim *exactly*.
2. **"A dry Rot"** — capitalised, named. Used in Parliament without explanation — not specialist vocabulary.
3. **Predates Mahon (1778) by four years** as the earliest timber application.
4. The term migrated from naval cordage (Gray 1725) to building timber by 1774.
5. Total building destruction — "the Whole is become dangerous... absolutely necessary the same should be re-built."

Source: *Journals of the House of Commons*, session Nov 1772 – Sept 1774. ECCO, Gale doc HREXSB527305661.

**1776 — David Hartley, *An account of some experiments made with the fire-plates***

The most analytically important passage found in this research:

> "With respect to the accident which sometimes happens to the timbers of an House, and which is **commonly called by the term Dry-Rot**, and which we now impute to the want of a circulation of air; **we do not know the real cause of it**. Nothing is more common than to find the **Dry-Rot, as it is called, in wainscots, which certainly cannot be from want of air**. I conceive the most material cause of the Dry-Rot, **or rather what should be called the Wet-Rot**, to be from adventitious damp steaming upon the timbers, as upon Ground-floors, where there may chance to be some spring or damp soil underneath."

Significance:
1. **"Commonly called"** — the term was established vernacular by 1776, not new or specialist.
2. **"We do not know the real cause"** — honest admission that the prevailing theory (want of air circulation) is insufficient.
3. **The wainscot puzzle** — dry rot appears in wainscoting (open rooms), where lack of ventilation cannot be the cause. This is someone observing *Serpula lacrymans* spreading beyond the damp zone and being baffled by it.
4. **Challenges the name** — "or rather what should be called the Wet-Rot." Hartley thinks the name is wrong because the cause is moisture. He's right about the initiating cause but the name describes the *result* (dry, crumbling timber).
5. **Proposes damp as cause** — "adventitious damp steaming upon the timbers" from ground-floors. Same observation as Hales 1758.
6. **This is the Hartley that Mann cites in 1778** — the fireproofing inventor whose work led to the dry rot / fireproofing connection.

Source: Hartley, David. *An account of some experiments made with the fire-plates* (S.n., [1776]). ECCO, Gale doc CB0127804405.

**1768-1773 — Ships saturated with salt; vessels "lined with fungi" (from Britton 1875)**:
- Britton's retrospective account states: "Between 1768 and 1773 a practice prevailed of saturating ships with common salt" — during Pringle's presidency
- "several instances are attested of **vessels whose interiors were lined with fungi** having all traces of the plant destroyed by accidental or intentional sinking in the sea"
- This confirms that **fungi were visibly growing inside ship interiors during the 1770s** — and that salt/seawater immersion was used as a remedy
- Treat with caution as a secondary source (Britton writing in 1875), but the specificity of the date range and the detail about salt saturation suggest a reliable underlying source

**1778 — Charles Lord Viscount Mahon, "Description of a most effectual Method of securing Buildings against Fire" (jstor-106351)**
- **First use of "dry-rot" in the Philosophical Transactions**
- Read July 2, 1778 — during Pringle's final year as President
- Context: "very particular attention must be paid to the rough plaster-work and to the sand being **most perfectly dry** before the boards are laid, **for fear of the dry-rot**; of which however there is no kind of danger, when this precaution is made use of."
- The term is used without definition — it is already known
- About **buildings**, not ships
- Prevention: keeping materials dry before enclosure

### Phase 4: Related Material Preservation (1778-1781)

**1778 — David Macbride, "An improved Method of tanning Leather" (jstor-106318)**
- Communicated BY Pringle to the Royal Society
- "The use of tanning is two-fold; first, to **preserve the leather from rotting**; and, secondly, to render it impervious to water"
- Oak-bark tannins as antiseptic for organic matter — directly relevant chemistry to timber preservation
- The parallel with timber treatment (oak bark, astringents) is implicit

**1779 — Edward King, "Account of a Petrefaction" (jstor-106405)**
- Addressed to Pringle as PRS
- Iron solutions harden and preserve stone from "waste and decay"
- Proposes iron washes for preserving Portland stone carvings and buildings
- Framework: understanding material decay to prevent it

**1781 — Henry Smeathman, "Some Account of the Termites" (jstor-106519)**
- Termites as "the greatest plagues of both Indies"
- Describes hollow destruction of wooden structures: "scarce credible but to those who have seen it"
- Biological agent destroying wood — but tropical insects, not fungi

### Phase 4a-i: The Society of Arts Gold Medal (1783)

**1783 — Society for the Encouragement of Arts, Manufactures, and Commerce, *Transactions*, vol. 3**

> "**Preventing the Dry Rot in Timber.** To the person who shall **discover to the Society, the cause** of the Dry Rot in Timber, and disclose a certain **method of prevention, superior to any hitherto known**; **the gold medal**. The accounts of the causes and methods of prevention, confirmed by repeated experiments to be produced to the Society on or before the **second Tuesday in December, 1785**."

The Society of Arts — headquartered in the **Adelphi** (built by the Adam brothers, 1768-1772) — offered its highest award for solving the dry rot problem. The cause was still unknown in 1783 and existing remedies were inadequate. The December 1785 deadline coincides exactly with the Keate v. Adams trial where Banks testified.

The Adelphi thread: the Adam brothers built the Adelphi → were sued for dry rot in another building (1785) → their own Adelphi building got dry rot (c.1800, treated by Dr. Higgins). The Society of Arts was offering prizes for solving a problem that would soon attack their own building.

The full run of annual premium lists was processed through OLMoCR. Results:

| **1759-1780** | **NO** — 20+ years of premiums, no dry rot category |
| **1781-1782** | Missing volumes — critical gap |
| **1783** | First appearance (*Transactions* vol. 3) |
| **1784** | Yes — item 109, gold medal |
| **1785** | Yes — item 111, gold medal |

The prize was **never awarded** through at least 1789 — the Society kept re-advertising with escalating incentives. Nobody could explain the cause or offer a satisfactory prevention. The 1772 *Premiums offered* list contains prizes for planting timber (acorns, chestnuts, elm, Scotch fir) but nothing on timber preservation or decay. The Society went from **no awareness** of dry rot (1759-1780) to **gold medal urgency** (1783-1789) in a few years.

### Phase 4a-ii: Mann's French Memoir (October 1778)

**October 1778 — M. l'Abbé Mann, "Memoire," *Observations sur la Physique, sur l'Histoire Naturelle et sur les Arts*, vol. XII, pp. 249+**

In a memoir on building materials published in a leading French scientific journal, Théodore Augustin Mann (a Benedictine monk, FRS, and member of the Imperial Academy of Sciences at Brussels) introduces the English term to a French audience:

> "ce que les anglois nomment **Dry-rot**, ou **Pourriture sèche**"
> ("what the English call Dry-rot, or dry putrefaction")

The context is a discussion of moisture in construction planks and the risk of rot.

Significance:
1. **October 1778** — the same year as Mahon's Phil Trans paper (read July 1778), making these the two earliest documented uses of the term
2. **Mann glosses it as an English term** — "ce que les anglois nomment" — he's introducing an established English concept to French readers. The disease was named in England first.
3. **"Pourriture sèche"** became the standard French term for dry rot
4. **Mann was connected to Joseph Banks and the Royal Society** — FRS, published in Phil Trans (1779, "A Treatise on Rivers and Canals," communicated by Banks). The international scientific network was sharing knowledge about dry rot.
5. **Continental circulation** — the problem was being discussed across the Channel by 1778, consistent with Wulfen classifying *Serpula lacrymans* in Austria in 1781.
6. The context is **building materials** (planks, moisture, construction) — consistent with all other 1778 evidence.

Source: Mann, M. l'Abbé. "Memoire." *Observations sur la Physique, sur l'Histoire Naturelle et sur les Arts*, vol. XII, Oct. 1778, pp. 249+. Nineteenth Century Collections Online, Gale.

### Phase 4a-iii: The Times Legal Case (1785)

**1785 — *The Times*, 13 December 1785, p. 3: "Exchequer. KEATE against Mell. ADAMS"**

A lawsuit in the Court of Exchequer:

> "This was an action brought by Mr. George Keate, of Charlotte-street, plaintiff, against Mell. Adams, of the Adelphi, defendants, for some failures that had happened by **the common and dry rot** in his house, alleged to be occasioned by the use of Linnets's cement on the walls of a new building, executed under Mell. Adam's directions, as architects and builders."

The arbitrators — **Sir William Chambers** and Mr. Paine, both leading architects — found no cause of action and declared the Adams (likely the firm of the famous Robert Adam) had built with "great professional skill and ability."

Significance:
1. **"The common AND dry rot"** — the two types of rot are distinguished as separate damage categories in a legal context. By 1785, "dry rot" was a recognised, named condition distinct from ordinary rot.
2. **Earliest newspaper use of "dry rot" found in this research** — seven years after Mahon's 1778 Phil Trans mention.
3. **A NEW building** — not a neglected old structure. Dry rot appearing in new construction, attributed to cement trapping moisture on walls. This is exactly the mechanism Mahon warned about in 1778 and is characteristic of *Serpula lacrymans* behaviour: moisture trapped behind plaster enabling the fungus to attack structural timber.
4. **High-profile defendants** — the Adam brothers (architects of the Adelphi) and Sir William Chambers (architect of Somerset House) as arbitrator, indicating dry rot was a serious concern at the highest levels of the building profession.
5. **In *The Times*** — confirms the term was in mainstream public discourse, not just specialist vocabulary.

**Follow-up report, 16 December 1785, p. 2**: The *Times* published a further account of the trial, noting that "The **dry rot**, which has become so [prevalent] in [parts] of this Metropolis" was the subject of attention. Critically, **Sir Joseph Banks, President of the Royal Society**, testified as an expert witness on the nature of wood decay and oak timber. Banks was PRS from 1778 to 1820 — the entire period of the dry rot crisis — yet no Phil Trans articles on dry rot appeared during his presidency. He was consulted as an expert in court but the subject never entered the Transactions. This gap between Banks's personal knowledge and the Phil Trans record tells us something important about what the Royal Society chose to publish.

Sources:
- "Exchequer." *Times*, 13 Dec. 1785, p. 3. The Times Digital Archive, Gale doc CS50725261.
- "London." *Times*, 16 Dec. 1785, p. 2. The Times Digital Archive, Gale doc CS33816976.

### Phase 4b: Withering's Botanical Identification (1792)

**1792 — William Withering, *A Botanical Arrangement of All the Vegetables Naturally Growing in Great Britain*, 2nd edition, vol. 3, p.421**

Withering's entry:
> **BOLE'TUS** *lachrymans*. [WITHER.]—Tubes white. *dry-rot*
> Pileus or cap coloured, wrinkled, reticulated, with a broad, white, cottony edge.

This is the **earliest known inclusion of *Boletus lachrymans* in a British botanical reference**, with "dry-rot" given as the English common name. Withering claims authority for the determination ("[WITHER.]").

Significance:
1. **Absent from the 1st edition (1776), but the 1776 edition was NOT weak on fungi.** Withering's 1776 fungi section covers ~50 Agaricus species, ~13 Boletus species, plus Hydnum, Phallus, Helvella, Peziza, Clavaria, and Lycoperdon. He includes multiple species growing on rotten wood, in cellars, on tree trunks, and on buildings ("On old ruinous cottages," "On rotten boards," "in cellars, ships, &c."). He was clearly paying attention to wood-decay fungi — and dry rot was not among them.
2. **Added between 1776 and 1792** — precisely the period of hypothesised *Serpula* spread. The absence from 1776 and presence in 1792 is strong evidence that *B. lachrymans* became recognisable in Britain during this sixteen-year window.
3. **Five years before Sowerby** (1797), who later criticised Withering for having "never saw the plant" — suggesting Withering worked from Continental descriptions (Jacquin, Wulfen) rather than personal specimens
4. **"Dry-rot" as standard common name** in a mainstream botanical reference by 1792
5. The morphological description (wrinkled, reticulated pileus with broad white cottony edge) matches *Serpula lacrymans*
6. Bridges the gap between Wulfen's Continental classification (1781) and Sowerby's British illustrations (1797)

**Withering 1776 and the Pepys Toadstools**:

Withering's 1776 fungi section was checked against Pepys's description of fist-sized toadstools growing in damp, unventilated ship holds. No convincing match was found. His wood-decay fungi are either too small (Bunch Mushroom), wrong shape (bracket fungi, clubs), or wrong habitat. The fungi most likely responsible for Pepys's toadstools — *Donkioporia expansa* (oak mazegill), large *Pholiota* species, or *Coniophora puteana* (cellar fungus) — do not appear clearly in the 1776 edition.

Species in Withering 1776 growing on wood in damp/enclosed conditions:
- Purple-stalked Mushroom (No. 6) — "On rotten wood," clusters, tawny/brown — possibly *Hypholoma* but described as small
- Comb Agaric (No. 55, *Pectinatus*) — "On rotten wood, in cellars, &c." — leathery and toothed, not toadstool-shaped
- Black Clubtop (Clavaria No. 6) — explicitly "in cellars, ships, &c." — but small finger-like structures
- Touchwood Spunk (Boletus No. 3) — large brackets on trees, but hard, not fleshy toadstools

This reinforces two points:
1. **Mycology in 1776 was still patchy.** Withering knew fungi on wood existed but couldn't classify everything. The absence of *B. lachrymans* should not be over-interpreted as proof of biological absence on its own — but combined with the terminological and Phil Trans evidence, the pattern is consistent.
2. **The built environment was under-surveyed.** Withering was a Midlands physician botanising in woods and pastures, not inspecting naval dockyards or building foundations. The fungi of enclosed built environments (ship holds, under floors, between walls) were poorly known before the dry rot crisis forced attention to them. This is itself significant: it took a *new, aggressive, economically devastating organism* — dry rot — to make British naturalists look at building fungi seriously.

Source: William Withering, *A Botanical Arrangement*, 1st ed. (London, 1776); 2nd ed., vol. 3 (London, 1792), p.421.

### Phase 4c: Sowerby's Mycological Illustrations (1797)

**1797 — James Sowerby, *Coloured Figures of English Fungi or Mushrooms* (MDCCXCVII)**

Sowerby's Tab. CCLXXXIX (*Boletus hybridus*) contains the first known British mycological identification of dry rot fungi by name:

> "This Boletus has many characters in common with the **B. lachrymans** and B. Medulla-panis. It is generally found growing horizontally **under rotten floors** attached by its back, **spreading in large patches, forming more or less broad ramifications, often inosculating**, of a **cottony substance** like the above mentioned, which are **commonly known by the name of Dry-rot**."

Key plates and descriptions:

**Tab. CXIII — *Boletus lachrymans* ("Common Dry-rot")**:
> "**Much too common in England**, taking possession even of the **bond timber in houses**, and often attached by the back **under stair-cases**, &c. In damp places the fructification is very frequent... Their colour varies from **yellow to orange, or a bright red brown**. The whole fructification often forms a circle from one to six or eight inches in diameter, surrounded with an outer substance, **tender, and pithy or cottony, of a pale brown**. The upper part is commonly clothed with a **white mucor**. **This pithy substance, without fructification, is often found by itself, and is very dry; whence the English name of dry rot**: yet as the fructification is seldom without **drops of water resembling tears**, the Latin name *lachrymans*, or weeping, has been given."
>
> Footnote: "*The best way to guard against this evil, is to introduce a free circulation of fresh air, and avoid building in damp situations.*"

**Tab. CCLXXXIX — *Boletus hybridus*** (now *Fibroporia vaillantii* (DC.) Parmasto 1968):
> "has many characters in common with the B. lachrymans and B. Medulla-panis... found growing horizontally **under rotten floors**... spreading in large patches, forming more or less broad ramifications, often inosculating, of a cottony substance like the above mentioned, which are **commonly known by the name of Dry-rot**."

Note: In 1797 Sowerby describes *B. hybridus* from **building** contexts (under rotten floors). By 1812 he will find the same species on HMS Queen Charlotte — but "seldom" accompanied by *B. lachrymans*. The organism Britton later calls *Polyporus hybridus* ("the dry rot of our oak-built vessels") is this same species: *Fibroporia vaillantii*, a brown-rot polypore of conifers (order Polyporales), taxonomically unrelated to *Serpula lacrymans* (order Boletales).

**Tab. CCCCIII — *Merulius lamellosus***:
> Found "at the bottom of a rotten bin in a cellar at Charlton House, Kent" — described as having "a strong resemblance to **Boletus lachrymans, or Common Dry-rot**, tab. 113, in some states."

This is significant because:
1. **Sowerby explains the etymology of "dry rot"**: the mycelium without fruiting bodies "is very dry; whence the English name"
2. **He explains the Latin name**: "drops of water resembling tears" = *lachrymans* (weeping)
3. **"Much too common in England"** — already a widespread pest by 1797
4. The description is **textbook modern *Serpula lacrymans***: orange-brown fruiting body, white cottony mycelium, pale brown pithy margin, water droplets on fructification
5. **All specimens from buildings** (houses, stair-cases, floors, cellars), not ships
6. Sowerby prescribes **ventilation** — "introduce a free circulation of fresh air" — the same remedy Hales proposed for ships in 1755
7. References **Jacquin** (*Misc. Austr.*) and **Dickson** (*fasc. i*) — placing it in established Continental and British mycological literature
8. By 1797, **British mycologists had identified the organism, classified it, explained its names, and connected it to the vernacular** — the scientific identification of dry rot was complete

Source: James Sowerby, *Coloured Figures of English Fungi or Mushrooms* (London, 1797). Internet Archive: colouredfigureso00sowe.

### Phase 4c: The Gentleman's Magazine Evidence (1797)

**1797 — Gentleman's Magazine, vol. 67, p.209**: "On the Causes and Cure of the Dry Rot"

Two letters published in March 1797 — one from Sunbury, one from Dublin — discuss dry rot as a well-known disease of buildings. The Sunbury correspondent debates whether damp is the primary cause, discusses "the two frequent causes of the generation of dry-rot," and describes seeking remedies. The Dublin letter discusses dry rot in the Little House of Lords in Dublin.

This is significant because:
1. **"Dry rot" is a fully established disease name by 1797** — 19 years before M'William's 1818 treatise
2. The context is **buildings** (houses, the Dublin Parliament), not ships
3. Correspondents are debating **causes** (damp vs. dryness) and **cures** — the disease is well enough known to generate public debate
4. The Gentleman's Magazine was a widely-read general periodical — this was not specialist knowledge
5. It bridges the gap between Mahon's casual 1778 mention and the post-1800 crisis literature

### Phase 5a: Sowerby's Navy Board Commission and the Two-Organism Distinction (1812-1814)

**1812-1814 — James Sowerby, Reports and Correspondence with the Navy Board**

The full Sowerby archive (NHM B95-B98, 442 pages) has now been read and analyzed (April 2026). The formal published correspondence (34 pages) was already in the KG; the field notebook (B96, 47 pages), rough drafts (B97, 153 pages), fair copies (B98, 78 pages), and Navy Board correspondence (B95, 133 pages) contain substantial evidence not in the formal report.

**Commission and Inspection**

John Knowles at the Navy Office initiated the commission, having "frequently stated both at the Admiralty and this Board the necessity of taking your opinion on the subject of the dry-rot in the Queen Charlotte" (B95, 15 July 1812). Sowerby was summoned to the Navy Office on 15 July, commissioned on 17 July, and travelled to Plymouth with Lionel Lukin on 21 July, inspecting the Queen Charlotte on 24-25 July. His report of 4 August was adopted fleet-wide by 28 August — the Admiralty ordering repairs "according to your suggestions without further reference to Mr Lukin's reports." Lukin had advocated drying timber by intense heat from stoves placed in the hold and on decks; Sowerby rejected this as likely to "increase the temperature of the moisture & consequently the fermentation without removing it." HMS Vigo, "a ship built about three years since and heated according to Mr Lukins plan," was already "in a bad state from the dry rot" (Knowles, B95) — evidence that Lukin's method was actively destructive.

**The Formal Fungi List and What It Conceals**

The published report names four species on the Queen Charlotte: *Boletus hybridus* (EF Tab. 289), *Xylostroma giganteum* (EF Tab. 358), *Auricularia pulverulenta* (EF Tab. 214), and — crucially — "very little remains of *Boletus Lachrymans* E.F. 113, the best known Dryrot." But the field notebook (B96) contains **no species identifications at all**. The identification was done retrospectively in London, comparing specimens or drawings against Sowerby's own *English Fungi* plates. The earliest draft (B97) reads "perhaps Boletus hybridus" — the identification was uncertain. The formal report's taxonomic precision is a considered judgment, not a field observation.

**The Full Dockyard Species List (~25 Species)**

B98 page 25 contains a complete inventory of fungi found across the dockyard — in timber being brought to the dock, on piles in the dock, on ship frames, and on finished ships. This list contains approximately 25 species, compared to the 4 named in the published report. *Boletus Lachrymans* is explicitly marked **"seldom."** The full list includes *Mucor mucedo* (commencement of rot), *Boletus versicolor* (plenty), *Auricularia reflexa* (plenty), *Tremella mesenterica*, *Clavaria cornea*, *Reticularia Lycoperdon*, and many others. Items marked "G.C." (= Queen Charlotte) include *B. hybridus* (289), *B. medulla panis* (326, "plenty"), and *Tylostoma giganteum* (358, "plenty" — Sowerby's alternative name for *Xylostroma giganteum*).

This is, as far as we know, the **earliest systematic mycological survey of a naval dockyard** — and it shows that "dry rot" in ships was a syndrome caused by a community of ~25 fungal species, not a single organism.

**Sowerby's Own Two-Organism Distinction**

A draft passage in B98 (page 16) that did not make it into the formal report reveals Sowerby explicitly distinguishing ship fungi from building dry rot:

> "the latter most voracious devourers of wood [*Boletus hybridus* and *Xylostroma giganteum*]... The well known *Boletus lachrymans* common dry rot **found more in damp building on shore** is only to be compared with them"

This is the earliest known statement by a mycologist explicitly separating ship timber decay from building dry rot — predating Britton's (1875) distinction between "*Polyporus hybridus* (the dry rot of our oak-built vessels)" and "*Merulius lachrymans* (the Dry Rot)" by over 60 years. Britton was restating what Sowerby had already observed in his field notes.

**Humidity-Fungi Correlation Scale**

Sowerby constructed a quantitative scale (B97 page 78, B98 page 45) correlating specific fungi to specific humidity thresholds on his hygrometer (where 1000° = saturation):

- 300°: *Mucor* (first indicator of rot)
- 600°: *Xylostroma simplex*
- 700°: Intermediate species; *Boletus Lachrymans* likely to grow
- 800°: *Boletus medulla panis* and *Xylostroma gigantea* ("Oak Leather")

The Queen Charlotte's hold stood at 828.6° — **above** the *B. lachrymans* threshold (700-800°) and in the *Xylostroma* zone (800-900°). This is consistent with finding "very little" *B. lachrymans* but abundant *Xylostroma*: the ship's environment was too wet for dry rot but ideal for the oak-specific decay fungi. This is early quantitative mycological ecology — correlating environmental measurements to species-specific growth thresholds.

**The B. hybridus Taxonomy: Resolved**

Modern nomenclatural databases (GBIF, MycoDB, Index Fungorum, MycoBank) and Petersen's 1983 annotated index to Sowerby's plates (*Mycotaxon* 17: 148-230) all identify *Boletus hybridus* Sowerby (EF Tab. 289) as **Fibroporia vaillantii** (DC.) Parmasto 1968. The transfer chain: *B. hybridus* → *Polyporus hybridus* (Berkeley & Broome, c. 1860) → *Poria hybrida* (Cooke, 1886) → heterotypic synonym of *F. vaillantii*. This is a brown-rot polypore in the order **Polyporales** — taxonomically remote from *Serpula lacrymans* (order Boletales), which Sowerby classified as *Boletus lachrymans*. The two organisms differ at the ordinal level.

*Fibroporia vaillantii* is a well-documented building timber decay fungus — the "white pore fungus" or "mine fungus" of European timber pathology. It causes brown rot of **coniferous** wood (spruce, pine, fir). This creates a substrate paradox: Sowerby found it on HMS Queen Charlotte, nominally an oak-built warship. But Royal Navy ships used mixed timbers — oak for frames and hull planking, but **pine and fir for decking, masts, interior joinery, and ceiling planks**. Sowerby never explicitly says *B. hybridus* was on oak — he says it was on the ship. A question in the correspondence from co-inspector Lukin (8 October 1812) — "I think that you told me yesterday that the Queen Charlotte was built of *American Timber* — was it so?" — further complicates the substrate picture. The paradox is real but resolvable: *F. vaillantii* likely colonized the softwood components of the ship.

The previous suggestion in this knowledge graph that *B. hybridus* might be *Donkioporia expansa* (oak mazegill) was **incorrect**. *D. expansa* has a separate basionym (*Boletus expansus* Desm., 1823) and was never connected nomenclaturally to *B. hybridus*. Furthermore, *D. expansa* causes white rot (not brown rot) and has a "discreet" rather than spreading mycelium — a poor morphological match to Sowerby's "White rugged soft Fungi" with "broad ramifications."

**The Credit Dispute**

Sowerby was awarded 200 guineas (5 January 1814) but the Navy Board explicitly denied him credit for saving the Queen Charlotte: "we do not attach to you the merit of having saved the Queen Charlotte by your advice, all the defective materials having been taken out of that Ship at Plymouth when she was repaired there." Yet Sowerby insisted "she is now on service, providentially saved by my directions." The B95 correspondence reveals this was partly managed by Knowles and Seppings, who advised Sowerby to submit a memorial. By 1817, the relationship had soured: Seppings invited Sowerby to the Navy Office "up the private stairs to one of the model rooms to ask my opinion, privately" — but Sowerby, "thinking, unfair, under all circumstances... did not give my advice or opinion." He had submitted his expenses twice to Knowles, unpaid.

Sources: NHM B95-B98 archive, transcribed March 2026, fully analyzed April 2026. Published correspondence: *Reports and Correspondence between The Honourable Commissioners of His Majesty's Navy and James Sowerby F.L.S. &c. 1812* (34pp manuscript, British Museum Natural History stamp 26 Jun 1937).

### Phase 5b: Britton 1875 as Secondary Source — Use with Caution

Thomas Allen Britton's *A Treatise on the Origin, Progress, Prevention, and Cure of Dry Rot in Timber* (1875, Gutenberg: 55541) provides a comprehensive retrospective history but **must be read sceptically**. Key problems:

1. **Anachronistic application of "dry rot" to Pepys**: Britton paraphrases Pepys's 1680s observations and implicitly frames them as dry rot. Pepys never used the term. The 1680s decay was wet rot from neglect in damp conditions, not *Serpula lacrymans*.

2. **Continuous disease narrative**: Britton's chapter "On the Gradual Rise and Development of Dry Rot" frames the entire history as a single disease, when the evidence suggests the 1680s crisis (native brown rot fungi) and the 1780s+ crisis (*Serpula lacrymans*) involved different organisms.

However, Britton embeds **genuine primary evidence** not found elsewhere:

- **c.1798 Woolwich ship** (from *European Magazine*, Dec 1811): "the **orange and brown coloured fungi** were hanging, in the shape of **inverted cones**, from deck to deck." This description matches *Serpula lacrymans* fruiting bodies — the first we've found specifically on a ship.

- **Banks wine cask anecdote**: "A circumstance recorded by Sir Joseph Banks... a cask of wine confined for three years in a cellar... had leaked and vegetated in the form of immense fungi, which had filled the cellar, and borne upwards the empty wine cask." Another Banks/fungi connection; original source not yet identified.

- **Two-organism distinction**: Britton separates "*Merulius lachrymans* (often called *the* Dry Rot)" in buildings from "*Polyporus hybridus* (the dry rot of our **oak-built vessels**)" in ships — different fungi in different contexts.

- **Ship decay rates** (from Chapman 1817 and Pering 1812): 74-gun ships decaying in 5-7 years; ships of war "useless in five or six years"; fungi "growing so strong betwixt the timbers as to force a plank from the ship's side half an inch."

## Analysis

### What the Combined Evidence Tells Us

1. **1680s (Pepys)**: Timber decay in neglected naval ships is catastrophic, with visible fungal fruiting bodies ("Toad-stools as big as my Fists"). But this is wet rot in unventilated, damp conditions. Pepys — a Fellow and President of the Royal Society — treats the *type* of decay as unsurprising; only the *extent* is shocking. The cause is diagnosed as neglect, not disease. No named condition exists.

2. **1694 (Leeuwenhoek)**: Unexpected timber rot attributed to pre-existing "inward decay" in living trees — the old framework. No awareness of post-harvest fungal colonisation.

3. **1742-1755 (Sutton, Watson, Hales)**: Ship ventilation becomes a major topic. Hales (1755) explicitly identifies harbour ship timber decay as serious and ongoing, attributing it to "putrid, corroding air." This could mark the first institutional recognition of a worsening or changing situation — possibly the early phase of *Serpula* spread.

4. **1772-1778 (Pringle's presidency)**: The theoretical framework for understanding decay is dominated by Pringle's antiseptic chemistry. Cook, Priestley, Macbride all work within this framework. The TERM "dry-rot" appears (Mahon 1778) but only for buildings — used casually, without definition.

5. **The critical gap**: No one in the Phil Trans connects timber decay to fungal growth. British scientists have microscopes and are observing fungi on wood (Miles 1749, Woodward 1784), but never make the causal connection. This suggests either (a) catastrophic *Serpula* outbreaks hadn't yet forced the connection, or (b) the putrefaction framework was so dominant it precluded biological explanations.

6. **1781 (Wulfen)**: *Serpula lacrymans* is formally classified — outside Britain, by an Austrian botanist. The organism was being encountered by Continental naturalists.

7. **Post-1800**: The "dry rot" crisis explodes in published literature (M'William 1818, Knowles 1821), but the Phil Trans itself largely misses it — the crisis was primarily discussed in naval, architectural, and trade publications.

8. **1812 (Sowerby)**: The first scientific mycologist to inspect a naval ship finds a **multi-organism community** (~25 species), with the "best known Dryrot" (*B. lachrymans*) only "seldom" present. The primary ship attackers are *B. hybridus* (= *Fibroporia vaillantii*, a conifer-specialist polypore, likely on the ship's pine/fir components) and *Xylostroma giganteum* (oak-specific). Sowerby's humidity-fungi correlation shows the QC hold (828.6°) was in the *Xylostroma* zone, above the *B. lachrymans* threshold. His unpublished drafts explicitly distinguish ship fungi from building dry rot — an insight later restated by Britton (1875) and the 1911 *Encyclopædia Britannica*, but subsequently lost when all "dry rot" became equated with *Serpula lacrymans* alone.

### The Wet Rot / Dry Rot Distinction

The Pepys evidence establishes a crucial baseline — but the distinction between the 1680s crisis and the later dry rot crisis is **behavioural, not symptomatic**.

**The symptom is the same**: both native brown rot fungi and *Serpula lacrymans* produce brown cubical rot that reduces wood to dry powder. Multiple indigenous Western European fungi cause this — *Coniophora puteana* (cellar fungus), *Laetiporus sulphureus* (chicken of the woods, common on oak), *Fibroporia vaillantii* (mine fungus), *Fomitopsis pinicola* (red-belted conk). All digest cellulose while leaving lignin, causing wood to shrink, crack into cubes, and crumble. Pepys's "perish'd to powder" could be any of these.

**The behaviour is different**: native brown rot fungi require sustained high moisture and stop spreading when wood dries out. *Serpula lacrymans* is uniquely destructive because it:

- Grows thick rhizomorphs that **transport water** from damp areas to dry timber
- Can spread across **brick, masonry, and dry wood** to find new food sources
- Attacks timber that **appears dry** — the paradox that gave it its English name
- Is "apparently infectious" (1823 Britannica) — spreading in ways that seemed inexplicable before the organism was understood

**What changed between the 1680s and the 1770s** was not the symptom (powdery timber) but the behaviour of the decay. Pepys's ships rotted because they were neglected in damp conditions — stop the neglect, and the rot stops. The dry rot crisis of the late 18th century was characterised by decay appearing in places that *weren't* particularly damp: under stair-cases, between floors, in bond timber of well-maintained buildings. That novel, spreading, inexplicable behaviour is what forced the coining of a new term and eventually the identification of a new organism.

Sowerby captures the essence of this distinction in 1797: "This pithy substance, without fructification, is often found by itself, and **is very dry; whence the English name of dry rot**." The name describes the paradox: rot where there shouldn't be rot. That paradox is *Serpula lacrymans*.

The transition from Pepys's world (native brown rot in damp neglected ships) to the dry rot crisis (an invasive organism attacking dry timber in buildings and ships) is the period we are trying to map. The combined evidence suggests this transition occurred roughly 1750-1790, with Hales's 1755 alarm, Mahon's 1778 terminology, Wulfen's 1781 classification, and Withering's 1792 addition as key markers.

### The Multi-Organism Framework: Ships vs Buildings

Sowerby's 1812 archive introduces a crucial complication to the simple Serpula narrative. **The naval dry rot crisis and the building dry rot crisis involved different organisms.** His full dockyard survey documented ~25 species of fungi across timber yards, ship frames, and finished vessels. The dominant organisms on HMS Queen Charlotte were not *B. lachrymans* (building dry rot) but *Boletus hybridus* (now *Fibroporia vaillantii*, a brown-rot polypore of conifers) and *Xylostroma giganteum* (an oak-specific decay organism). *B. lachrymans* was "seldom" found.

Sowerby's humidity-fungi scale provides the ecological explanation. The QC hold (828.6°) was above the *B. lachrymans* growth threshold (700-800°) and in the *Xylostroma* zone (800-900°). In the saturated conditions of a ship's hold — far wetter than the enclosed but not waterlogged spaces of buildings — the building dry rot organism was outcompeted by organisms adapted to wetter environments.

This reframes the historical narrative in important ways:

1. **"Dry rot" was not one disease but a multi-organism syndrome stratified by humidity and substrate.** Within a single warship, three organisms occupied distinct ecological niches:

| Zone | Humidity | Timber | Primary Fungus |
|------|----------|--------|----------------|
| Hold / below waterline | 828-900° (saturated) | Oak frames, keelson | **Xylostroma giganteum** (oak-specific) |
| Between decks (damp, not wet) | 700-800° | Pitch pine ceiling planks, deal decking | **Boletus lachrymans / Serpula lacrymans** + **B. hybridus / Fibroporia vaillantii** (both conifer specialists) |
| Upper works (ventilated) | 400-600° | Oak hull planking | No significant fungal growth |

2. **Pitch pine was the critical substrate.** Sowerby singled out pitch pine as particularly vulnerable: its natural veins of turpentine confined moisture within the wood, creating microenvironments ideal for fungal colonization. He directed that any pitch pine to be replaced must be "perfectly cleared of every vestige of decay" and thoroughly dried. B97 page 88 confirms Xylostroma was found "in the pitch pine nearly related to it for its ravages." Both *F. vaillantii* and *S. lacrymans* are conifer specialists — and American pitch pine, used extensively between decks, was their primary food source. Sowerby strongly cautioned against deal (softwood/pine) generally, calling it "always comparatively soft and spongy" and noting its splinteriness made it "an unmerciful act" to use in warships. By contrast, he praised English oak as capable of becoming "nearly as hard as Iron by age and proper attention."

3. **Sowerby's "very little remains" of B. lachrymans must be read with a crucial caveat.** He inspected the QC after "great quantities of Timber had been removed." The between-decks zone — where B. lachrymans would have been most prevalent on the pine/deal components — was the most accessible area and likely stripped first. The Navy Board cited this stripping to deny him credit. His finding of "very little" B. lachrymans may partly reflect a removal bias: the most damaged softwood was gone before he arrived. The *Xylostroma* on oak in the deep hold would have been harder to access and later to remove, so it was still present for Sowerby to document.

4. **The building crisis and the between-decks zone were ecologically equivalent.** The damp-not-wet zone of a ship (700-800°) is the same humidity environment as an enclosed building interior where *Serpula lacrymans* thrives. When Sowerby wrote that B. lachrymans was "found more in damp building on shore" (B98 p.16), he was not saying it was absent from ships — he was recognizing that it favours the *damp* environment, which exists in both buildings and the between-decks zone. The building dry rot crisis (*S. lacrymans* on pine/fir construction) and the between-decks component of the naval crisis were the *same* disease on the *same* substrate in ecologically similar environments.

5. **Britton (1875) and the 1911 *Encyclopædia Britannica* captured a real distinction but oversimplified it.** Their separation of "*Merulius lachrymans* (the Dry Rot)" from "*Polyporus hybridus* (the dry rot of our oak-built vessels)" reflects genuine mycological differences that Sowerby had already observed in 1812. But the picture is more nuanced than a simple two-organism split: it was a three-organism community stratified by humidity within the ship, with *S. lacrymans* and *F. vaillantii* sharing the softwood between-decks niche while *Xylostroma* occupied the saturated oak structure below.

6. **The taxonomy confirms the distinction at the ordinal level.** *Fibroporia vaillantii* (order Polyporales) and *Serpula lacrymans* (order Boletales) are as distantly related as — in the compass artifact's analogy — "a cat and a whale within mammalian taxonomy." Yet both specialized in the same substrate (coniferous wood) in the same humidity zone (damp, not wet). This convergent ecology on divergent taxonomy is itself significant: the naval pine/deal was being attacked by at least two unrelated lineages of brown-rot fungi simultaneously.

### Independent Witnesses to the Multi-Organism Reality

Four independent observers, none relying on the others for their core observations, confirm the multi-organism picture:

**1. NAUTICUS (European Magazine, November 1811)** — A pseudonymous dockyard observer recalls fungi at Woolwich c.1798: "the orange and brown coloured fungi were hanging in the shape of inverted cones from deck to deck." This is the strongest morphological evidence for *Serpula lacrymans* on a warship — the colour ("orange and brown") and form ("inverted cones") match Sowerby's 1797 description of *B. lachrymans* exactly. The location — "from deck to deck" in fire-ships with pine trunks fitted between decks — is the pine zone. NAUTICUS also independently confirms pitch pine as the most vulnerable substrate: "this wood is the soonest destroyed by fungi, as these plants are nourished by the great quantity of resin contained in its numerous cells."

**2. Pering (Plymouth Dockyard, January 1812)** — A senior dockyard officer with nearly 30 years' experience, writing from the same yard where the QC was being repaired. Pering explicitly distinguishes fungi from dry rot as **different phenomena**: "fungus and dry-rot are not one and the same thing; for the latter does not require one-half of the moisture to keep it in a state of activity, that fungus does." He describes dry rot's contagious spreading behaviour: "by taking a small portion of dry-rot and scattering it in a confined spot, between the wood work, the timbers, instantly, contract the complaint." This is a pre-mycological observation of the wet rot / Serpula distinction by a man with no scientific training — he sees the ecological difference without knowing the taxonomy. He names no species; his vocabulary is generic ("fungus," "dry-rot," "vegetation"). But his ecological distinction is independently valid.

**3. Sowerby (July 1812)** — The only mycologist to inspect a warship. Found ~25 species across the dockyard, B. lachrymans "seldom." His unpublished drafts (B98 p.16) explicitly distinguish ship fungi from building dry rot.

**4. Knowles (Navy Office, 1812-1821)** — Secretary to the Committee of Surveyors. His 1821 book, Chapter VIII, provides independent ecological observation: "While the *Xylostroma giganteum*... is generally confined to the rotten wood which produced it, the *Boletus lachrymans* spreads with a wonderful rapidity." He confirms the QC dry rot was "found only above the orlop-deck" (the pine between-decks zone), that the QC was built with "Canada oak, and American pitch-pine timber," and — critically — that "dry-rot" had been "given indiscriminately to almost every description of rot, whether fungus was or was not present."

### Serpula Was Real: Against Overcorrecting the Two-Organism Model

It would be a mistake to read Sowerby's QC findings as evidence that *Serpula lacrymans* was unimportant to the naval crisis. Several strands of evidence confirm it was a major player:

1. **Knowles explicitly names it.** Writing to Sowerby before the QC inspection, Knowles states: "Many ships that have, to the best of my knowledge, been constructed of English Oak only, have also been destroyed by *Boletus lachrymans*." He names the Foudroyant (80 guns, Plymouth 1798) and the Ajax (74, built by contract 1798) — both "nearly destroyed" within three years. These were English oak ships, not American timber.

2. **Green timber was everywhere.** Under wartime pressure — especially the St Vincent timber policy (1801-1804) — unseasoned oak was forced into service. Green oak has high moisture content and has not yet developed the tannin resistance of properly seasoned heartwood. It was ideal substrate for any decay fungus, including Serpula. The post-1800 spike in dry rot literature directly follows this policy failure.

3. **Buildings and ships shared the same crisis.** The same organism devastating London houses, churches, and public buildings in the 1790s-1810s was clearly also entering ships through their softwood components. The between-decks zone of a warship (damp pine/deal, 700-800° humidity) is ecologically identical to a building interior. There is no reason to think Serpula respected the boundary between land and sea.

4. **American pitch pine was especially vulnerable.** Knowles observed: "It is not only in this ship that Pitch-pine has proved more subject to dry rot than other wood, but also in the Bellepoule Frigate... timber grown in America is very subject to decomposition from that cause." American pitch pine — used extensively between decks — was the most susceptible substrate. Both *S. lacrymans* and *F. vaillantii* are conifer specialists that would have thrived on it.

5. **Sowerby himself called B. lachrymans "the best known Dryrot."** He didn't dismiss it. He found "very little remains" on a partially stripped ship in conditions too wet for Serpula's optimal range — but this is a specific finding about one ship at one moment, not a general claim about the naval crisis.

**The balanced synthesis:** Serpula lacrymans was the novel, aggressive element in the crisis — the invasive organism new to Britain, spreading through buildings and entering ships through their softwood components. But warships also harboured native decay fungi (*Xylostroma*, various *Auricularia* and *Boletus* species) that had always been present in damp ship holds — the same organisms Pepys saw in 1684. Sowerby's contribution was unique: he was the only person who actually identified fungi to species level, and he found a community of ~25 species where everyone else saw only "the dry rot." The naval crisis was multi-causal: Serpula invasion + native decay fungi + green timber + American pitch pine + poor ventilation + Lukin's counterproductive stove-heating = catastrophic fleet decay. No single organism and no single explanation is sufficient.

### Implications for the Serpula lacrymans Timeline

The Phil Trans evidence is consistent with the hypothesis that Serpula lacrymans arrived and spread aggressively in Britain during the second half of the 18th century:

- The **absence** of the term before 1778 and the absence of any description matching dry rot's distinctive symptoms (timber crumbling to dry dust, decay spreading through dry material, infectious progression) before mid-century supports a later arrival
- Hales's 1755 alarm about harbour ship decay could mark the early phase of the organism's spread
- Mahon's casual 1778 usage suggests the term was established by the 1770s at latest
- The 1823 Britannica claim that it was known "at some period of Sir John Pringle's presidency" is corroborated
- The explosion of dry rot literature post-1800 is consistent with a crisis that built through the late 18th century

### Limitations

- The Phil Trans is one publication — the term may appear earlier in Admiralty records, builder's manuals, Gentleman's Magazine, parliamentary papers, or trade literature
- OCR quality varies; some instances may be missed due to OCR errors
- The Phil Trans had particular editorial interests (natural philosophy, mathematics, medicine) that may have excluded practical trade knowledge about timber
- The absence of evidence is not evidence of absence

### Modern Scientific Context

The phylogeographic evidence (Kauserud et al. 2007, *Molecular Ecology* 16(16): 3350-3360) confirms:
- **Asian/Himalayan origin** of *Serpula lacrymans* — wild populations in montane coniferous forests at 2,000-4,000m in NE India, Yunnan, Japan
- **European populations show reduced genetic diversity** consistent with a founder event from Asian wild populations
- **Molecular clock divergence estimate**: 2,000-5,000 years between European "domestic" and Asian wild strains
- But the **dramatic expansion** in European buildings is post-medieval, associated with enclosed timber construction
- The organism's optimum temperature (~20-23°C) explains why it thrives in cool northern European buildings

This is consistent with our documentary evidence: the organism may have been present in Europe for centuries at low levels, but its catastrophic expansion in British buildings dates to the mid-to-late 18th century, driven by changes in construction practice (more enclosed timber, less ventilation, damp masonry in contact with wood).
