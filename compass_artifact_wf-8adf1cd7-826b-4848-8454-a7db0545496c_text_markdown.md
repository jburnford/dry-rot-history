# Sowerby's "Boletus hybridus" was a real species, not misidentified Serpula lacrymans

**Sowerby's *Boletus hybridus* (Tab. 289, c. 1803) represents a genuinely distinct fungal species that was never synonymized with *Serpula lacrymans*.** Modern taxonomy identifies it as *Fibroporia vaillantii* (DC.) Parmasto 1968, a brown-rot polypore in the order Polyporales — an entirely different fungal lineage from *S. lacrymans* (order Boletales). This finding vindicates Victorian mycologists like Britton (1875) who explicitly distinguished "the dry rot of our oak-built vessels" from the *Merulius lacrymans* attacking softwood buildings. However, the accepted synonymy introduces a significant paradox: *F. vaillantii* is overwhelmingly a conifer specialist, yet Sowerby and Britton described this fungus from oak ship timbers. This discrepancy, combined with the emergence of *Donkioporia expansa* as the recognized destroyer of oak structural timber, suggests the taxonomic identity of the organism that actually rotted British warships remains an open and researchable question.

## The nomenclatural record settles one question definitively

The taxonomic history of *Boletus hybridus* Sowerby can be traced through every major nomenclatural database. GBIF, MycoDB, Index Fungorum (via Wikimedia Commons concordance), and MycoBank all place it within the synonymy of **Fibroporia vaillantii**. The transfer chain runs: *Boletus hybridus* Sowerby (c. 1803) → *Polyporus hybridus* (Sowerby) Berkeley & Broome (c. 1860) → *Poria hybrida* (Sowerby) Cooke (1886) → heterotypic synonym of *Fibroporia vaillantii* (DC.) Parmasto (1968). The basionym of the accepted species is *Boletus vaillantii* DC. (1815), named after the French botanist Sébastien Vaillant.

Critically, **at no point in this chain was *Boletus hybridus* placed in *Merulius*, *Serpula*, or any merulioid genus**. Berkeley transferred it to *Polyporus*; Cooke moved it to *Poria*. Both are polypore genera in Polyporales, taxonomically remote from the Boletales where *Serpula lacrymans* resides. The two organisms differ not merely at the species level but at the ordinal level — roughly analogous to confusing a cat with a whale within mammalian taxonomy. The species was also never placed in *Donkioporia* or connected to *D. expansa*, whose basionym is the entirely separate *Boletus expansus* Desm. (1823).

Britton's (1875) placement in *Polyporus* was therefore taxonomically astute. In his *Treatise on the Origin, Progress, Prevention, and Cure of Dry Rot in Timber*, he wrote: "Another formidable fungi, which attacks oak in ships, is the *Polyporus hybridus* (the dry rot of our oak-built vessels). It is thus described by Berkeley: 'White, mycelium thick, forming a dense membrane, or creeping branched strings, hymenium breaking up into areæ, pores long, slender, minute.'" The 1911 *Encyclopædia Britannica* preserved this distinction verbatim: "The most formidable of the dry rot fungi is the species *Merulius lacrymans*, which is particularly destructive of **coniferous wood**; other species are *Polyporus hybridus*, which thrives in **oak-built ships**."

## Fibroporia vaillantii presents a substrate paradox

*Fibroporia vaillantii* (also widely known as *Antrodia vaillantii* in older literature) is a well-documented building timber decay fungus — the "white pore fungus" or "mine fungus" of British and European timber pathology. It causes **brown rot**, degrading cellulose while leaving modified lignin, producing the characteristic cuboidal cracking pattern. Its morphology matches Berkeley's description closely: white cottony mycelium forming dense membranes, thin flexible white strands, and resupinate fruiting bodies with small angular pores. In Polish building surveys it accounted for **13.8% of documented decay cases**, and in Norwegian roof beams the genus *Antrodia* (including *A. vaillantii*) was the most frequent at **17.3%**.

The problem is substrate. Every modern ecological study describes *F. vaillantii* as a **coniferous wood specialist**. It primarily colonizes spruce, pine, and fir. In a comprehensive North American survey, it ranked among the ten most prevalent fungi on conifers but was absent from the list for hardwoods. No published record documents it attacking oak specifically. Yet Sowerby described his specimen from ship timbers, and Britton explicitly called it the dry rot of oak-built vessels.

Three explanations could resolve this paradox. First, Royal Navy warships used mixed timbers — **oak for frames and hull planking, but pine and fir for decking, masts, interior joinery, and ceiling planks**. *F. vaillantii* could have colonized the softwood components while being described loosely as occurring "in ships." Second, the synonymy itself may rest on morphological similarity alone, without adequate ecological verification. Sowerby's original specimen and Berkeley's subsequent description were based on macroscopic characters — white mycelium, branching strands, minute pores — features shared by several resupinate polypores. Without molecular data or type specimen re-examination, the equation of *B. hybridus* with *F. vaillantii* remains a taxonomic hypothesis, not a proven identity. Third, ships' timbers subjected to prolonged saltwater exposure and bilge conditions may have had sufficiently altered extractive chemistry to permit colonization by fungi that would not normally attack sound oak heartwood.

## Serpula lacrymans can attack oak, but weakly

Modern mycological evidence confirms *S. lacrymans* is **not strictly restricted to softwoods**, though its preference is overwhelming. Haas et al. (2019), analyzing 645 fungal damage samples in Austrian buildings, documented oak (*Quercus* spp.) as the second most common wood type affected, after spruce. Laboratory mass loss data from Baar et al. (2020) shows recent oak heartwood loses only **~2.5% mass** to *S. lacrymans*, while subfossil oak with leached tannins loses about **4.9%** — compared with **36–69% mass loss** for spruce and pine. Oak heartwood earns Durability Class 2 ("durable") under EN 350 precisely because its phenolic extractives inhibit fungal enzymes.

Watkinson and Eastwood's definitive 2012 review in *Advances in Applied Microbiology* notes that "softwood is more vulnerable to attack than seasoned hardwood, as expected from the origin of the fungus in Montana and cool conifer forests." Balasundaram et al. (2021) in *The ISME Journal* describe *S. lacrymans* as a "niche specialist... with a strong substrate preference for spruce." Wild populations, first cultured from the Himalayas at **2,800–3,100 m** elevation by White et al. (1997), grow exclusively on coniferous wood.

This evidence makes *S. lacrymans* a plausible but improbable primary agent of severe oak decay in warships. It could colonize oak timbers opportunistically, especially where extractives were leached by water exposure, but the **2.5% mass loss on sound oak** versus the catastrophic structural failures described in naval records (HMS Queen Charlotte's repair costs exceeding construction costs within two years) suggests either additional organisms were involved or conditions in ships dramatically enhanced susceptibility.

## Donkioporia expansa: the modern oak decay specialist

The fungus modern building conservation identifies as the primary destroyer of oak structural timber is ***Donkioporia expansa*** (Desm.) Kotlaba & Pouzar 1973 — the "oak mazegill" or "Oak Rot." This organism causes **white rot** (degrading both lignin and cellulose), producing pale, stringy, fibrous wood rather than the dark cuboidal cracking of brown rot. It requires persistently wet conditions and is classified as a wet-rot agent. BCS Preservation states it "can cause more extensive damage to oak than any other fungus."

*D. expansa* has a critical ecological partnership with the **death watch beetle** (*Xestobium rufovillosum*), the most destructive insect pest of historic oak timber. Belmain et al. (2002) demonstrated that female beetles preferentially oviposit on oak pre-decayed by *D. expansa*, using fungal volatiles for host selection. This beetle-fungus association has been devastating for oak-framed historic buildings across Western Europe.

However, *D. expansa* does **not match Sowerby's morphological description** well. Its mycelium is flat, firmly adherent to the timber substrate, and does not spread far. Pottier et al. (2014) noted it is "characterized by a more discreet development" compared with *S. lacrymans*. The "cottony substance... spreading in large patches, forming broad ramifications, often inosculating" from the user's query better describes the extensive, free-spreading cotton-wool-like mycelium and thick rhizomorphs of *S. lacrymans* — or, indeed, the white cottony mycelium of *F. vaillantii*.

Furthermore, *D. expansa* was **never connected nomenclaturally to *Boletus hybridus***. Its own synonymy chain (from *Boletus expansus* Desm. through *Polyporus megaloporus*, *Poria expansa*, to *Donkioporia expansa*) is entirely separate. And its decay type — white rot versus the brown rot implied by the cuboid-cracking descriptions in historical dry rot literature — creates a fundamental mismatch with the "dry rot" label applied to ship timber decay.

## Evidence supports a multi-organism framework for historical "dry rot"

The most important finding for the historian is that **"dry rot" as understood in the 18th–19th century was not a single disease but a syndrome caused by multiple organisms on different substrates**. Sowerby's 1812 survey of "Fungi found in the Dockyards and Ships of Great Britain from Deptford to Plymouth" documented **23 species** in naval installations, of which *Merulius lacrymans* was only one. The *Nature* review of Ramsbottom's 1937 presidential address to the Essex Field Club notes there "can be little doubt that it was one of the chief agents of decay" — but the phrasing itself acknowledges others were present.

Modern building mycology formally distinguishes brown-rot fungi attacking softwoods (*S. lacrymans*, *Coniophora puteana*, *Antrodia/Fibroporia vaillantii*) from white-rot fungi attacking hardwoods (*Donkioporia expansa*, *Phellinus contiguus*). Singh (2020) in the *Journal of Building Survey, Appraisal & Valuation* codifies this distinction explicitly. The Austrian building survey by Haas et al. (2019) documented this pattern in practice: *Serpula* dominated softwood floors while *Donkioporia* infested heavily soaked hardwood beams.

For the naval context specifically, the conditions aboard warships — poor ventilation, high humidity, mixed timber species, and iron-induced chemical changes in wet oak — created an environment hospitable to multiple decay organisms simultaneously. The historical conflation of these under the single term "dry rot" obscured real biological diversity and substrate specificity. Britton and the *Encyclopædia Britannica*'s explicit distinction between *Merulius lacrymans* on conifers and *Polyporus hybridus* on oak represents a genuine mycological insight that was subsequently lost when all "dry rot" became popularly equated with *S. lacrymans* alone.

## A significant gap remains in the historical record

**No modern molecular study has revisited Sowerby's 23 dockyard fungi or examined preserved historic naval timber using DNA methods.** This represents the single largest gap in the scientific literature relevant to this question. Ramsbottom's 1937/1941 historical account (*Essex Naturalist* 25: 231–267) remains the most important secondary work, but it is narrative history, not taxonomic revision. Petersen (1983) published an annotated nomenclatural index to Sowerby's plates in *Mycotaxon* 17: 148–230, apparently equating Tab. 289 with *Fibroporia vaillantii*, but this appears to follow morphological convention rather than molecular verification.

Maritime archaeological studies of historic ships (Mary Rose, Nanhai No. 1) have focused on waterlogged timbers degraded by bacteria and soft-rot fungi under anaerobic conditions — a completely different decay regime from the above-waterline "dry rot" that devastated ships in service. No published molecular identification of *S. lacrymans*, *F. vaillantii*, or *D. expansa* from archaeological ship timbers was found. A PhD project at Cranfield University (2022) on HMS Victory's oak timbers represents a promising new avenue but has not yet published fungal identification results.

## Conclusion

The evidence converges on a clear finding: **Sowerby's *Boletus hybridus* was taxonomically distinct from *Serpula lacrymans* and was recognized as such by every mycologist who handled the name.** The accepted modern identity is *Fibroporia vaillantii*, a brown-rot polypore of coniferous timber. This placement, while nomenclaturally standard, creates an unresolved substrate paradox with the original description from oak ship timbers that no modern study has addressed.

For the historian of imperial timber supply chains, three insights emerge. First, the Victorian-era distinction between "ship dry rot" (oak, *Polyporus hybridus*) and "building dry rot" (softwood, *Merulius lacrymans*) reflects genuine biological differences that modern mycology supports in principle, even if the specific identities need molecular re-examination. Second, the catastrophic decay of oak naval timber was likely caused by a community of organisms — potentially including *Donkioporia expansa* (oak white rot), *Fibroporia vaillantii* (if on softwood components), and *Serpula lacrymans* (opportunistically on degraded oak) — rather than any single species. Third, the equation of all "dry rot" with *Serpula lacrymans* in popular and even some scientific discourse is historically anachronistic and biologically reductive. Sowerby himself knew better: he found 23 species in the dockyards, and his *Boletus hybridus* was deliberately named as a separate entity. The full mycological story of Britain's rotting warships remains, remarkably, untold by modern science.
